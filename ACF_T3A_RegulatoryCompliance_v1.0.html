Executive Summary

ABC Consumer Finance Vietnam (ABC) operates as a State Bank of Vietnam (SBV)-licensed consumer finance company, 100% owned by ABC Financial Service Co., Ltd. (Japan). ABC's regulatory landscape is governed by a complex and rapidly evolving set of laws, circulars, and decrees spanning consumer lending, data protection, cybersecurity, anti-money laundering, and consumer rights. This assessment maps ABC's current operations -- including cash loans, installment loans, the VPBank NEO 3-party agent banking arrangement, and pipeline products (credit cards, BNPL, 2-wheeler loans) -- against the full Vietnamese regulatory framework. 1.2 Circular 43/2016/TT-NHNN -- Consumer Lending Rules (Core Regulation)

Circular 43/2016/TT-NHNN is the primary regulation governing consumer lending by finance companies in Vietnam. It was amended by Circular 18/2019/TT-NHNN. Key provisions applicable to ACF:

1.2.1 Scope and Definitions

  • Consumer loan is defined as a loan granted by a finance company to a customer (individual) for the purpose of paying for goods and services to serve the customer's daily needs (Article 3).
  • Consumer loans do not include loans for business purposes, loans secured by real estate, or loans for purchasing real estate.
  • Finance companies must establish and maintain internal regulations on consumer lending, approved by the Board of Directors, covering: loan assessment criteria, lending procedures, interest rate ranges, fee schedules, and collection processes.

1.2.2 Interest Rate Regulation

  • Interest rate calculation: Consumer loan interest rates fixed by a finance company must conform to SBV regulations on lending transactions of credit institutions (Article 9, referencing Circular 39/2016/TT-NHNN).
  • Civil Code cap: Under Article 468 of the Civil Code 2015, the maximum lending interest rate for civil transactions is 20% per annum on the principal balance (applicable when parties agree on interest rates outside the framework of credit institution lending).
  • Finance company rates: While the 20%/year Civil Code cap does not directly apply to licensed credit institutions (which are governed by SBV-set rates), SBV periodically sets maximum short-term lending rates. Consumer finance companies typically charge 20-50% APR depending on product and risk profile.
  • Late payment interest: Capped at 150% of the contractual interest rate per Article 13, Circular 39/2016/TT-NHNN.
  • Transparency requirement: Finance companies must clearly disclose to borrowers: (a) the interest rate calculation method, (b) total cost of borrowing, (c) fee schedules, and (d) penalty interest provisions before loan disbursement (Article 10).

1.2.3 Fee Disclosure and Borrower Protection

  • Finance companies must provide borrowers with a written loan agreement containing all material terms prior to disbursement.
  • Fees that may be charged include: loan origination fees, loan management fees, early repayment fees, and late payment fees. All fees must be disclosed in advance.
  • Finance companies are prohibited from charging hidden fees or bundling non-disclosed charges.
  • Borrowers have the right to early repayment with a maximum early repayment fee set by the finance company's internal regulations.

1.2.4 Collection Practices

  • Circular 43 requires finance companies to establish collection procedures that are "reasonable and not contrary to law."
  • Collection communications must not harass, threaten, or mislead borrowers.
  • Collection agents acting on behalf of finance companies must be properly authorized and supervised.
  • ABC utilizes Debt Collection 4.0 system and engages external collection agencies (VNPost, AEON Super Market, Viettel Store, Glam Boutique, Payoo, AEON Wellness per EY report).

1.2.5 Amendments via Circular 18/2019/TT-NHNN

  • Enhanced disclosure requirements for total cost of borrowing.
  • Strengthened requirements for finance companies to provide clear information on interest rate calculi"Law on Credit Institutions 2024" — National Assembly of Vietnamation methods.
  • Additional consumer protection provisions regarding right to information and complaint resolution.

1.3 Law on Credit Institutions 2024 (Law No. 32/2024/QH15)

The 2024 Law on Credit Institutions replaced the 2010 Law (No. 47/2010/QH12) and its 2017 amendments. Effective July 1, 2024, it introduces significant changes relevant to ACF:

1.3.1 Restructured Finance Company Classification

  • General finance companies: Broad operational scope including factoring, buyer credit, finance lease.
  • Specialized finance companies: Focus on specific activities. Consumer finance companies fall under this category.
  • Consumer finance companies (specialized) can: (a) accept term deposits of VND 100 million or above, (b) issue certificates of deposit, (c) borrow from other credit institutions, (d) provide consumer loans, (e) issue credit cards (with SBV approval), and (f) provide payment services linked to consumer lending activities.

1.3.2 Ownership Rules (100% Foreign Ownership)

  • ABC, as a 100% subsidiary of ABC Financial Service Japan, operates under the foreign ownership provisions.
  • Under the 2024 Law, the Government may approve foreign ownership above standard caps for purposes of restructuring credit institutions.
  • Individual ownership cap reduced to 5% of charter capital; organizational ownership capped at 10%.
  • These caps do not apply to existing 100% foreign-owned entities that were licensed before the law took effect, subject to SBV confirmation.
  • ABC's 100% ABC Japan ownership is grandfathered under its existing SBV license but must comply with ongoing reporting requirements.

1.3.3 Lending Limits (Critical Change)

  • Single borrower limit: Reduced from 25% to 15% of equity for a single customer (Article 136).
  • Related party limit: Reduced from 50% to 25% of equity for a single customer and related persons.
  • Transition period: Credit institutions have until January 1, 2029 to comply with the new limits.
  • ABC impact: Must review large exposures and ensure portfolio concentration complies by 2029 deadline.

1.3.4 Early Intervention and Special Control

  • Chapter IX introduces a comprehensive early intervention framework.
  • SBV can place a credit institution under early intervention if: capital adequacy ratio falls below minimum, liquidity difficulties arise, or governance failures occur.
  • Special control provisions allow SBV to appoint administrators and restrict operations.

1.3.5 Related Party Transaction Restrictions

  • Enhanced disclosure of related party transactions.
  • Prohibitions on preferential lending to related parties.
  • Board members, executives, and major shareholders face stricter conflict-of-interest rules.
  • ABC must ensure all transactions with ABC Group entities (Japan, retail stores, malls) are at arm's length and properly disclosed.

1.5 Capital Adequacy Requirements

1.5.1 Current Requirements

  • Minimum Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR): 8% for consumer finance companies.
  • CAR = (Tier 1 Capital + Tier 2 Capital) / Risk-Weighted Assets.
  • Tier 1 capital includes: charter capital, retained earnings, reserves.
  • Tier 2 capital includes: subordinated debt, revaluation reserves (with limits).
  • Risk weights: consumer unsecured loans typically carry 100-150% risk weight.

1.5.3 Foreign Ownership Capital Requirements

  • As a 100% foreign-owned entity, ABC must maintain minimum charter capital of VND 500 billion.
  • Parent company (ABC Financial Service Japan) must demonstrate financial capacity to support the subsidiary.
  • Cross-border capital movements require SBV and State Bank of Vietnam (FOREX management) approval.
  • Dividend repatriation is permitted after meeting all capital requirements and tax obligations.

1.6 Anti-Money Laundering / Counter-Terrorism Financing

1.6.1 Law on Anti-Money Laundering 2022 (Law No. 14/2022/QH15)

The AML Law 2022 (effective March 1, 2023) significantly expanded Vietnam's AML framework:

  • Expanded definition: Money laundering now includes acts by individuals and organizations seeking to legitimize property obtained from illegal sources, covering both financial and non-financial sectors.
  • Reporting entities: Consumer finance companies are designated reporting entities under the law.
  • Customer Due Diligence (CDD): Mandatory KYC measures for all customers, enhanced due diligence (EDD) for high-risk customers, politically exposed persons (PEPs), and unusual transactions.
  • Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs): Must be filed within 48 hours of detection with SBV's Anti-Money Laundering Department.
  • Large cash transaction reporting: Transactions of VND 300 million or above must be reported.
  • Record retention: CDD records must be maintained for at least 5 years after the business relationship ends.
  • Risk-based approach: Reporting entities must conduct institutional money laundering risk assessments and maintain risk-based AML programs.
